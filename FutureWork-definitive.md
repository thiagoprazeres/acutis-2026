# Future Work / v3

## Carlo Acutis, Luciano Floridi, and the ethics of digital trust

This is not a roadmap item.

This is not a backlog note.

This is a door left open.

If v1 helped clarify how faith circulates, and v2 helped clarify how faith signifies, then v3 points toward a deeper question: what becomes of faith, memory, and trust when digital life is no longer only about publishing, but about generation, simulation, acceleration, and contested truth?

That future question deserves a different horizon.

It deserves Luciano Floridi's guidance.

Not because Carlo Acutis needs to be modernized, but because his work can now be read with greater precision. Carlo was a programmer and webdesigner who used technical systems not to maximize attention, but to organize access to sacred memory with legibility, structure, and trust. What he built was not merely a devotional website. He built a disciplined digital mediation of meaning.

Under Floridi's guidance, that gesture becomes even clearer.

Carlo can be read as a pioneering figure in the ethics of digital information: someone who acted within the infosphere not to exploit it, but to care for it. He did not treat information as noise, spectacle, or extractive material. He treated it as something that could serve human formation, public credibility, and spiritual orientation.

That is why v3 exists as future work.

Not to replace what has already been done, but to ask the next necessary question.

## Central thesis

**v3 proposes Carlo Acutis as a pioneering figure of digital information ethics: a programmer and webdesigner who used technical systems not to maximize attention, but to organize trustworthy access to sacred memory.**

Read through Floridi, Carlo’s work becomes more than digital evangelization. It becomes a case of the infosphere serving human and spiritual formation instead of extraction, spectacle, or acceleration.

## Why this future work matters

The digital world has changed.

The problem is no longer only how to publish meaningful content online.

The problem is how to preserve trust, provenance, legibility, and dignity in an environment flooded with content, optimized for distraction, and increasingly shaped by generative systems.

This is why Carlo matters now with new intensity.

He stands as a counter-model.

He catalogued instead of inflating.
He curated instead of accelerating.
He organized instead of overwhelming.
He designed for intelligibility, not compulsion.

That is not a minor detail of style.

That is a moral architecture.

## The inheritance of v3

Carlo remains the center.

Hohpe still explains architecture and integration.

Peirce still explains sign, interpretation, and semiosis.

Floridi adds the ethical and philosophical layer: infosphere, information quality, human dignity, moral responsibility, and the question of what kind of digital world our systems are helping to build.

So the line of thought becomes clearer:

- Hohpe helps explain how Carlo connected layers.
- Peirce helps explain how Carlo organized meaning.
- Floridi helps explain why that work matters in a damaged informational environment.

## Proposed arc

### 1. Carlo and the infosphere

Carlo’s catalog should be read not only as a devotional archive, but as an intervention in the digital environment itself.

He did not merely place Catholic content online.

He helped shape a zone of the infosphere in which sacred memory could remain ordered, accessible, and trustworthy.

### 2. Trustworthy information as care

Cataloging is not neutral.

To select, organize, verify, and present is already to care.

In Carlo’s work, source discipline, public legibility, and careful presentation appear not as technical afterthoughts, but as ethical acts. Information quality becomes a form of responsibility.

### 3. The sacred against attention extraction

Much of digital culture is built around distraction, acceleration, and behavioral capture.

Carlo’s work points in another direction.

He used digital tools to deepen attention instead of fragmenting it. He built toward reverence, not compulsion. In that sense, his project offers a quiet but serious counter-model to environments built for endless capture.

### 4. From digital mediation to information ethics

Floridi allows us to ask the larger question:

What kind of world does Carlo’s work imply?

A world in which technology serves memory.
A world in which design serves trust.
A world in which public access does not require the collapse of depth.
A world in which information can still be treated as part of human formation.

### 5. A proposal for future work

This is where the future turns personal.

The next step does not need to be immediate.

It does not need to be forced.

It does not need to be inherited as obligation.

It can remain what it should be: a serious question waiting for the right time.

Luciano Floridi helps name it.
