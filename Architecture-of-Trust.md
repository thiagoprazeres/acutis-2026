# Architecture of Trust / v3 Dossier

## Carlo Acutis, Luciano Floridi, and the digital ethics of memory

This document is not the public v3 text.

It is the research dossier behind it.

The public site already established the decisive line: Carlo Acutis was a programmer and webdesigner who gave digital form to a dispersed body of Eucharistic witness. He cataloged. The catalog became exhibition. The exhibition became site. By 2026, the same mission continues in mobile form.

That line remains the center.

v3 should not move away from Carlo. It should ask what his work makes possible now, in an environment shaped by generative systems, synthetic media, unstable provenance, and attention markets that reward speed more than meaning.

The answer is not a larger metaphor. It is an architecture of public trust.

## Central Thesis

Carlo Acutis can be read as an early figure of digital information ethics: a programmer and webdesigner who used technical systems not to maximize attention, but to organize trustworthy access to sacred memory.

Luciano Floridi helps name the ethical horizon of that gesture. His vocabulary of the infosphere, semantic capital, information quality, and information ethics gives v3 a way to ask why Carlo's digital work matters beyond Catholic media history.

Floridi does not replace Carlo.

Floridi gives us a language for the world Carlo was already entering.

## The Line That Must Stay Intact

The v3 argument depends on a simple continuity:

**catalog -> exhibition -> site -> mobile app**

That sequence is not a product roadmap. It is a mission becoming legible across media.

Carlo began by cataloging Eucharistic miracles approved by the Church. The work became an international exhibition. The exhibition was made navigable through the web. In February 2026, Vatican News reported a new mobile application dedicated to the Eucharistic miracles cataloged by Carlo Acutis.

The app matters because it proves continuity. It does not become the protagonist. It shows that Carlo's original intuition still generates new interfaces.

The form changes.

The mission remains.

## What Carlo Contributes

Carlo's contribution is not reducible to technical talent, religious devotion, or youthful symbolism.

It is the synthesis.

He understood that faith also needs structure, language, and circulation. He saw that sacred memory could remain scattered across archives, local traditions, books, images, and ecclesial records unless someone gave it a public path.

His work did five things at once:

- It organized dispersed testimony.
- It made sources publicly navigable.
- It translated local memory into a shared interface.
- It gave the visitor a path for interpretation.
- It treated digital access as a form of care.

That is why Carlo belongs at the center of an architecture of trust. He did not use the web as a place for spectacle. He used it as a place where credibility, memory, and meaning could circulate together.

## What Floridi Adds

Floridi gives v3 its philosophical vocabulary.

The infosphere names the informational environment in which human beings now live, act, remember, and decide. It is not merely "online space." It is the layered environment where digital, social, cultural, institutional, and personal information shape one another.

Semantic capital names the meaningful inheritance that a culture produces, receives, curates, and passes forward: language, memory, art, testimony, doctrine, knowledge, stories, and forms of orientation.

Information ethics asks how the infosphere can be cared for rather than degraded. It shifts the question from "Can this be published?" to "What does this do to the informational environment we share?"

Read through Floridi, Carlo's work becomes more than digital evangelization. It becomes a case of the infosphere being enriched rather than exploited.

Carlo took a body of sacred memory and increased its legibility. He preserved semantic capital instead of diluting it. He used technical form to help meaning survive circulation.

## The Architecture of Public Trust

An architecture of trust is not a slogan. It is a set of design obligations.

For v3, those obligations should be concrete:

1. **Keep Carlo as the subject.** Every technical or philosophical layer must return to his work: cataloging, exhibition, site, app, and Eucharistic witness.
2. **Preserve provenance.** Sacred, historical, and educational material should carry visible source discipline: where it came from, who maintains it, when it was accessed, and what changed.
3. **Design for interpretation.** Interface should not flood the visitor with content. It should guide attention through context, sequence, source, and meaning.
4. **Combine human judgment with technical credentials.** Metadata can help. Signatures can help. Standards can help. None of them replace editorial responsibility.
5. **Protect dignity.** Religious memory should not be converted into engagement bait, surveillance data, or devotional monetization without boundaries.
6. **Make access generous.** The point is not elite verification for specialists only. The point is public legibility without flattening depth.

This is the bridge from Carlo to Floridi: technology becomes trustworthy when it preserves meaning, clarifies provenance, and serves human formation.

## Technical Instruments

The technical layer belongs inside the architecture, but it is not the architecture itself.

C2PA Content Credentials can document provenance for images, video, documents, and AI-involved media. They can help a user see who created or changed a file, what tool was used, and whether a credential remains intact.

W3C PROV can model provenance more generally: agents, entities, activities, and relationships between them. It is useful for archives, research repositories, datasets, and institutional records.

Signed source registers can help institutions publish canonical lists of trusted materials, maintainers, publication dates, access dates, and update history.

Archival metadata can preserve the ordinary details that make memory accountable: title, institution, location, event date, rights, language, and source relation.

Human review remains mandatory. A signed file can still contain bad interpretation. A credential can prove origin without proving wisdom. A provenance system can document a chain of custody without guaranteeing that the chain serves truth well.

The v3 principle should be strict:

**Provenance supports trust. It does not automate trust.**

## Educational Direction

The most important future audience may not be technologists.

It may be young people learning how to live inside the infosphere without surrendering attention, judgment, memory, or dignity.

That makes Carlo especially useful. He is not an abstract ethics case. He is a concrete figure who used the technology of his time constructively.

An educational v3 could teach:

- how to verify sources without becoming cynical
- how to use digital tools without being consumed by them
- how to distinguish provenance from popularity
- how to treat information as care, not raw material
- how to build systems that serve memory and formation

This is also where Francisco belongs in the future of the project: not as someone handed an obligation, but as someone invited into a question.

How should technology serve memory, trust, faith, and human formation in the next generation?

That question is the real inheritance.

## Research Agenda

The future work should remain focused. v3 does not need to become a general ethics platform.

The strongest research path is narrow and defensible:

- **Carlo and information ethics:** Read Carlo's cataloging work as an intervention in the infosphere.
- **Semantic capital and sacred memory:** Treat Eucharistic testimony as inherited meaning that requires care during digital circulation.
- **Provenance and public trust:** Study how source discipline, content credentials, and editorial transparency change user confidence.
- **Interface and interpretation:** Explore how design can guide meaning without manipulating the visitor.
- **Education for the next generation:** Turn Carlo's example into practical digital ethics for young people and families.

The work can expand later. It should begin with Carlo.

## Risks and Boundaries

There are real risks.

The first is technical overconfidence. C2PA, W3C PROV, signatures, hashes, and metadata can strengthen provenance, but they cannot decide what is spiritually or morally adequate.

The second is philosophical overreach. Floridi helps name the informational environment, but not every dimension of faith can be reduced to information. Prayer, encounter, mystery, and conversion must not be treated as data problems.

The third is commercialization. A project about trust can easily become a brand about trust. That would invert Carlo's lesson. The architecture should serve public credibility and human dignity, not devotional capture.

The fourth is centralization. If only a few institutions can certify credibility, trust may become gatekeeping. v3 should consider plural, auditable, and transparent forms of stewardship.

The fifth is access. A trust architecture that ordinary users cannot understand will not serve them. Public trust requires public legibility.

These boundaries should be kept visible from the beginning.
